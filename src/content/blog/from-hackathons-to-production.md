I spent a good part of university helping run DeltaHacks, McMaster's largest
hackathon. At the time it felt like a detour from "real" engineering. A
decade into my career, I think it was some of the best leadership training I
ever got.

## Constraints are a gift

A hackathon is a fixed-scope, fixed-deadline project with volunteer staff and
a hard launch date. You cannot slip the weekend. That forces the same
discipline production teams need: ruthless prioritization, clear ownership,
and a plan for when things go wrong — because something always goes wrong at
2 a.m. on demo day.

## People ship projects

The hardest problems organizing a 500-person event were never technical.
They were about keeping a team of volunteers motivated through months of
unglamorous prep work. Production engineering is the same: the systems that
stay reliable are the ones owned by teams where people feel trusted and know
why their work matters.

## Demos are a forcing function

Every hackathon project gets demoed, working or not. I try to keep that
energy in my day job — show the thing, running, early and often. A demo
cuts through status-report fog like nothing else.

If you get the chance to help run something — a hackathon, a meetup, a
conference — take it. It's leadership reps you can't get from a sprint board.
